2 · TTL — Time to Live
How Long Clients Cache a DNS Record
High TTL (e.g. 24 hours)
  • Cheaper — fewer Route 53 queries
  • Slow to update — clients cache old record
  • Risk: changing IP takes hours to propagate
Low TTL (e.g. 60 seconds)
  • More expensive — more queries per second
  • Records change fast — clients pick up quickly
  • Use before planned DNS changes
Exam Strategy: Before making a DNS change, lower the TTL, wait for the high TTL to expire so all clients have refreshed, then make the change. After propagation is confirmed, raise TTL back up.
Exception: TTL is mandatory for all record types except Alias records, which have their TTL managed automatically by Route 53.

4 · Health Checks
3 Types of Route 53 Health Checks
1. Endpoint
Directly monitors a public resource (ALB, EC2 public IP). About 15 global health checkers run it. Passes if >18% report healthy.
2. Calculated
Combines up to 256 child health checks using OR / AND / NOT logic. Monitor aggregated status of a complex system.
3. CloudWatch Alarm
Monitors a CloudWatch Alarm state. The only way to health-check private resources inside VPC.
Check Interval: 30 seconds (standard) or 10 seconds (fast — higher cost). Healthy threshold: >18% of global checkers reporting healthy.
Security Group Rule Required: Route 53 health checkers are external. You must allow their IP ranges in your security group for HTTP/HTTPS/TCP checks to work.

6 · 3rd Party Domains
Domain Registrar ≠ DNS Service

You can register a domain at any registrar (GoDaddy, Namecheap, etc.) and still use Route 53 as the DNS service. The key is updating the NS (Name Server) records at the registrar.

Route 53 provides 4 name servers when you create a hosted zone. These are the NS records you paste into your domain registrar's dashboard to delegate DNS resolution to Route 53.
